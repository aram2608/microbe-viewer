Coevolution

Evolutionary Arms Race

Host and parasite evolve in lockstep — each new defense met with a new evasion, driving perpetual escalation.

CRISPR vs. phage: a microbial arms race in real time.

The CRISPR-Cas system gives bacteria an adaptive memory of past phage infections. But phages do not sit still, experimental evolution shows that phages rapidly evolve mutations in protospacer sequences to escape CRISPR recognition, while bacteria acquire new spacers targeting these escape mutants. This cycle can be observed directly in serial transfer experiments over days.

Common and colleagues demonstrated this reciprocal coevolution in Streptococcus thermophilus and its lytic phage over 30 days, showing that both CRISPR spacer acquisition and phage escape mutations occurred continuously. Notably, the arms race was asymmetric: bacteria accumulated defenses faster than phages could escape, eventually driving phage extinction, revealing a limit to what even a rapidly evolving parasite can sustain.

  • System: CRISPR-Cas vs. phage
  • Dynamics: Reciprocal coevolution
  • Outcome: Often phage extinction

Antigenic variation: staying one step ahead of immune memory.

For parasites that infect long-lived hosts with adaptive immunity, the arms race takes a different form. Rather than evolving new strains across generations, some parasites switch surface antigens within a single infection. Plasmodium falciparum encodes ~60 var genes for its major surface antigen PfEMP1 and expresses them one at a time, cycling through the repertoire to stay ahead of antibody responses.

This strategy is a direct evolutionary response to immune memory: a host that has seen one antigen variant is not protected against the next. The result is chronic, relapsing infection. Antigenic variation of this kind has evolved independently in trypanosomes, Borrelia, and influenza, suggesting it is a common solution to the problem of adaptive host immunity.

  • Strategy: Surface antigen switching
  • Example: P. falciparum var genes
  • Effect: Chronic infection

The Red Queen: why the race never ends.

The Red Queen hypothesis proposes that hosts and parasites are locked in perpetual coevolution where neither side gains a lasting advantage. Any host mutation that improves resistance is eventually countered by a parasite mutation that restores virulence, and vice versa.

This dynamic has broad consequences for evolution beyond host-parasite systems. It is one proposed driver of the evolution of sexual reproduction: genetically diverse offspring produced sexually are harder for parasites to track than genetically identical asexual clones. The arms race, in other words, may be one reason sex exists at all.

  • Concept: Red Queen hypothesis
  • Consequence: Sexual reproduction
  • Scale: Evolutionary time
