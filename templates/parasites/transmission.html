Routes of Spread

Transmission

How microbial parasites move between hosts — from direct predation to vector-borne journeys across continents.

Bacterial predation: Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus and direct invasion.

Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus is a small, fast-swimming predator that identifies prey cells by their outer membrane composition, drills through, and replicates inside the periplasm. This lifecycle is entirely intracellular and obligately dependent on a living host.

The genomic basis for this lifestyle, identified from the HD100 genome, includes an unusually large arsenal of hydrolytic enzymes for digesting host macromolecules, and dedicated chemotaxis machinery for prey detection. As such, transmission in this instance only requires proximity to their prey.

  • Host: Gram-negative bacteria
  • Entry: Periplasmic invasion
  • Transmission: Direct contact

Plant pathogens: hijacking roots and leaves.

Plant-infecting microbes face a different transmission problem, their hosts cannot move. Many spread through soil via water flow, through insect vectors, or as airborne spores. Phytophthora infestans, the oomycete responsible for the Irish potato famine , produces zoospores that swim through saturated soils and can disperse long distances in wind-carried sporangia.

Once on a leaf surface, it deploys RXLR effector proteins into plant cells to suppress immunity before the cell has had a chance to mount a defense. Transmission between fields can happen in hours under the right moisture conditions.

  • Host: Plants
  • Entry: Stomata and wounds
  • Transmission: Spores, water, wind

Waterborne and vector-borne routes in human parasites.

Many of the most globally significant microbial parasites rely on environmental intermediates or animal vectors for transmission. These indirect routes allow parasites to persist even when host density is low, and they introduce extra complexity into control efforts. Notable species include Cryptosporidium spp., Giardia intestinalis, and Toxoplasma gondii, with wastewater epidemiology testing as a proposed method of control. Understanding transmission routes is one of the core goals of epidemiology and is directly tied to how outbreaks are managed. Interrupting the transmission chain, whether by vector control, water treatment, or quarantine, remains the most effective tool available in the absence of targeted treatments.

  • Route: Water, food, vectors
  • Examples: Malaria, cholera
