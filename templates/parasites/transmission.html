Routes of Spread

Transmission

How microbial parasites move between hosts, from direct predation to vector-borne journeys across continents.

Bacterial predation: Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus and direct invasion.

Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus is a small, Gram-negative predator that identifies prey cells by their outer membrane composition and drills through and then replicates in the periplasm (Mookherjee & Jurkevitch, 2022). This lifecycle is entirely intracellular and dependent on a living host (Pierpont et al., 2025). A specialized lysozyme is required for exit that deacetylates the prey's peptidoglycan in order to create a distinction from the prey and the Bdellovibrio itself, allowing it to employ specialized "exit-enzymes" (Harding et al., 2020).

  • Host: Gram-negative bacteria
  • Entry: Periplasmic invasion
  • Transmission: Contact-dependent

Plant pathogens.

Plant-infecting microbes face a different transmission problem: their hosts cannot move. Many spread through soil via water flow, through insect vectors, or as airborne spores. Phytophthora infestans, the oomycete responsible for the Irish potato famine (Coomber et al., 2024), produces zoospores that swim through saturated soils and can disperse long distances in wind-carried sporangia.

Once on a leaf surface, it deploys RXLR effector proteins (Boutemy et al., 2011) into plant cells to suppress immunity before the cell has had a chance to mount a defense. Transmission between fields can happen in hours under the right moisture conditions.

  • Host: Plants
  • Entry: Stomata and wounds
  • Transmission: Spores, water, wind

Waterborne and vector-borne routes in human parasites.

Many of the most globally significant microbial parasites rely on environmental intermediates or animal vectors for transmission (Turner et al., 2021, Rizzoli et al., 2019). These indirect routes allow parasites to persist even when host density is low, and they introduce extra complexity into control efforts. Notable species include Cryptosporidium spp., Giardia intestinalis, and Toxoplasma gondii, with wastewater epidemiology testing (Puchades-Colera et al., 2025) as a proposed method of control. Understanding transmission routes is one of the core goals of epidemiology and is directly tied to how outbreaks are managed. Interrupting the transmission chain by vector control, water treatment, or quarantine remains the most effective tool available in the absence of targeted treatments (Institute of Medicine (US) Forum on Microbial Threats, 2008).

  • Route: Water, food, vectors
  • Examples: Malaria, cholera
