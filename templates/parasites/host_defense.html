Immune Strategies

Host Defense

Hosts have evolved layered defenses against microbial parasites, and parasites have evolved ways to get around them.

Plant immunity: a two-tiered system of detection and escalation.

Plants lack mobile immune cells, so every cell must be capable of defending itself. The first layer of plant immunity, pattern-triggered immunity (PTI), relies on surface receptors that detect conserved microbial features such as flagellin or chitin fragments. When these are recognized, the cell triggers a signaling cascade that activates antimicrobial responses.

Successful pathogens deploy effector proteins to suppress PTI, requiring plants to evolve a second layer, effector-triggered immunity (ETI), which is oftentimes a stronger and more localized response.

  • Layer 1: PTI — pattern recognition
  • Layer 2: ETI — effector detection
  • Response: Hypersensitive cell death

Bacterial defense: CRISPR-Cas as an adaptive immune system.

Bacteria face constant predation by bacteriophages and have evolved multiple layers of defense in response. CRISPR-Cas systems are the most sophisticated of these: they function as an adaptive immune memory, storing short sequences from past viral infections and using them to recognize and destroy matching DNA in future encounters.

When a bacterium survives a phage infection, the CRISPR machinery can integrate a short fragment of the phage genome, called a spacer, into the bacterial chromosome. On reinfection, the spacer is transcribed into guide RNA that directs the Cas nuclease to the matching phage sequence and cuts it. This system is heritable, giving daughter cells a record of their ancestors' infections.

  • Host: Bacteria and Archaea
  • Target: Phage DNA/RNA
  • Memory: Spacer acquisition

Human immunity and evasion by intracellular parasites.

The human immune system deploys both innate and adaptive responses against microbial parasites. Innate responses are fast and general, phagocytes engulf and destroy pathogens, while cytokines coordinate inflammation. Adaptive responses are slower but targeted: T and B cells recognize specific antigens and generate long-lived memory.

Intracellular parasites have evolved strategies to survive inside the very cells designed to kill them. Some avoid the lysosome, others interfere with antigen presentation, and some, like Plasmodium, undergo rapid antigenic variation to stay ahead of antibody responses. These evasion strategies are the result of millions of years of coevolution.

  • Innate: Fast, non-specific
  • Adaptive: Slow, targeted
  • Evasion: Variation, hiding
