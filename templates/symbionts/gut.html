Microbial Community

Gut Microbiota

Trillions of microbes colonize the human gut, shaping digestion, immunity, and metabolism.

Escherichia coli: A familiar gut resident with an outsized reputation.

E. coli is best known as a pathogen, but the vast majority of strains are harmless commensals that colonize the gut within hours of birth. Commensal strains compete with pathogens for nutrients and attachment sites, providing colonization resistance to the host. This process has been shown to be important for particularly troublesome diseases, such as colitis (Escribano-Vazquez et al., 2019). This relationship shines a spotlight on fecal transplants as treatments, and the importance of maintaining a healthy supply of commensal E. coli in our gut microbiomes.

Lactobacillus: Lactic acid producers that shape the gut environment.

Lactobacillus species ferment sugars to lactic acid, lowering local pH and inhibiting less acid-tolerant competitors. Members of this genus are also found in fermented foods and are among the most widely studied probiotic microorganisms. These bacteria have shown promise in helping regulate bowel diseases such as IBD (Fu et al., 2021) and colitis (Jia et al., 2020), demonstrating their broad importance and the benefit of including probiotics in a healthy diet.

Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron: A versatile carbohydrate specialist.

Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron encodes an unusually large repertoire of glycoside hydrolases (Ndeh et al., 2025), allowing it to break down complex plant polysaccharides the host cannot digest on its own. The genomic flexibility of Bacteroides species makes them a model for understanding how the gut microbiome adapts to dietary changes (Sonnenburg et al., 2010).
