Root Associations

Plant Partners

Soil microbes form intimate partnerships with plant roots, supplying nitrogen and nutrients in exchange for carbon.

Sinorhizobium (Ensifer) meliloti: Fixing nitrogen for alfalfa and beyond.

Sinorhizobium (Ensifer) meliloti is a soil bacterium that infects the roots of legumes like alfalfa, inducing the formation of nodules where it converts atmospheric N₂ into ammonia the plant can use. Its complex genome encodes a rich signaling toolkit for recognizing compatible host plants, and recent evidence suggests a surprisingly minimal subset of genes is sufficient to establish a symbiosis (Geddes et al., 2021). Adding to the intrigue, "cheater" strains that contribute little fixed nitrogen can persist in communities alongside cooperative partners, raising questions about how hosts enforce cooperation and how symbionts behave in complex soil environments. These dynamics place S. meliloti squarely in the conversation around the burgeoning field of sociomicrobiology (Greenberg, 2011).

Frankia: Nitrogen-fixer for plants that legumes left behind.

Frankia is a filamentous actinobacterium that forms nitrogen-fixing nodules on the roots of non-legume plants called actinorhizal hosts, including alder, casuarina, and sea buckthorn. Unlike rhizobia, which depend entirely on plant tissue to survive, Frankia can also fix nitrogen in free-living culture thanks to its protective hopanoid layer (Berry et al., 1993), which shields nitrogenase from oxygen and gives the bacterium unusual flexibility as both a soil microbe and a symbiont. These bacteria are also particularly resilient, tolerating elevated concentrations of salt (Ngom et al., 2016), copper (Rehan et al., 2014), and a variety of other compounds that would inhibit less hardy organisms.

Rhizophagus irregularis: The ancient fungal partner most plants rely on.

Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi like Rhizophagus irregularis colonize the roots of around 70% of land plant species (Pons et al., 2020). The fungus extends its hyphae far beyond the root zone, dramatically increasing phosphorus uptake (Qi et al., 2022). For the most part, it depends on the host for lipids, making it an obligate biotroph (Tamayo et al., 2025).
