Plant Partners

Soil microbes form intimate partnerships with plant roots, supplying nitrogen and nutrients in exchange for carbon.

Sinorhizobium meliloti: Fixing nitrogen for alfalfa and beyond.

Placeholder text. Sinorhizobium meliloti is a soil bacterium that infects the roots of legumes like alfalfa, inducing the formation of nodules where it converts atmospheric N₂ into ammonia the plant can use. Its tripartite genome encodes an unusually complex signaling toolkit for recognizing compatible host plants.

Frankia: Nitrogen-fixer for plants that legumes left behind.

Placeholder text. Frankia is a filamentous actinobacterium that forms nitrogen-fixing nodules on non-legume plants called actinorhizal plants, including alder and sea buckthorn. Unlike rhizobia, Frankia can also fix nitrogen in free-living culture, giving it unusual flexibility as both a soil microbe and a symbiont.

Rhizophagus irregularis: The ancient fungal partner most plants rely on.

Placeholder text. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi like Rhizophagus irregularis colonize the roots of over 80% of land plant species. The fungus extends its hyphae far beyond the root zone, dramatically increasing phosphorus uptake. Its genome lacks fatty acid synthesis genes entirely — it depends on the host for lipids, making the partnership obligate on the fungal side.
