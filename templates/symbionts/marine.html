Marine Symbionts

Microbial partnerships in the ocean sustain some of the most productive ecosystems on Earth.

Coral and Symbiodinium: An endangered combo.

Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor but support roughly 25% (Hoek & Bayoumi, 2017) of all marine species. The foundation of this productivity is a symbiosis between coral animals and photosynthetic dinoflagellates called Symbiodiniaceae. The algae live inside coral cells and supply a part of the coral's energy through photosynthesis (Matthews et al., 2023), while the coral provides shelter and inorganic nutrients in return.

This partnership is sensitive to thermal stress. When water temperatures rise, corals expel their algal partners in a process called bleaching (Baird et al., 2009). Without the algae, the coral loses its color and its primary energy source. Prolonged bleaching leads to starvation and death. Understanding the genetic diversity of Symbiodiniaceae and what determines thermal tolerance is an active area of research with direct implications for reef conservation.

  • Domain: Eukaryota
  • Type: Dinoflagellate
  • Role: Photosymbiont
  • Threat: Coral bleaching
