Salt Tolerance

Halophiles

Microbes adapted to extreme salinity, from salt flats to hypersaline lakes.

Halobacterium salinarum: A salty diva.

Halobacterium salinarum is well known for its purple color, resulting from the production of bacteriorhodopsin. This protein is an important light-driven ion pump that helps drive the production of chemical energy. This pump has roused a fair amount of scientific intrigue given its simplicity, making it one of the best understood pumps (Eichler, 2023) of its kind. H. salinarum is energetically flexible, capable of growing as an aerobic chemoorganotroph or anaerobically relying on the bacteriorhodopsin pump. Interestingly, the proposed oxidative phosphorylation (Gonzalez et al., 2009) pathway has demonstrated differences from the traditional and better studied pathways in E. coli and the mitochondria. Furthermore, the genome itself is remarkably plastic with its large number of insertion elements, a feature thought to drive rapid adaptation to fluctuating saline environments (Eichler, 2023).

Haloferax volcanii: The salt lover for the lab.

Haloferax volcanii is a unique archaeal species given that it has seen a rich development of a molecular toolkit and it's easy to culture (Pohlschroder et al, 2025), making it an ideal model organism for extreme environments. It was first isolated from the Dead Sea in the 1970s and named after Dr. Volcani, quickly gaining traction in the lab after the development of a transforming protocol (Cline & Doolittle, 1987). Progress was then required in developing selective markers in order to select for desirable mutants, with the discovery of mevinolin and its derivatives as a great boon towards the continued development of the molecular toolkit. Along with knockout lines and genome sequencing, this interesting archaeon shows the progression of how niche organisms can quickly gain traction as model organisms, and how the development of a strong molecular toolkit is a necessary prerequisite towards this status.

Salinibacter ruber: The bacterium cosplaying as an archaeon.

When one imagines an extremophile, they often conjure up an image of archaea, given their impressive reputations for tolerating every extreme environment Earth can throw at them. Salinibacter ruber is a particularly tenacious species of bacteria that bucks the trend, demonstrating tolerance can be attributed to a broader range of organisms than is often thought. It is a rod-shaped bacterium with a unique orangish hue that grows optimally at 20-30% salt concentrations, making it one of the more tolerant of halophiles. It has several other interesting features such as its well-studied lipids (Baronio et al., 2010), Bacterioruberin and salinixanthin carotenoid production (Jehlička et al., 2013), and its importance for evolutionary studies given its identity crisis as a bacteria that dreams of being an archaea.
