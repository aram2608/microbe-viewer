Heat Adaptation

Thermophiles

Organisms adapted to high temperatures, from geothermal pools to deep-sea vents.

Pyrococcus furiosus: A fast-growing model for life at extreme heat.

Pyrococcus furiosus was discovered in 1986 by Stetter and colleagues (Fiala & Stetter, 1986), when archaea were often called archaebacteria. The name "furiosus" refers to its rapid movement and growth. Unlike many thermophiles, it can use starch as a food source and grows only in oxygen-free conditions. It also has an unusual glycolytic pathway (Straub et al., 2020). Its best-known contribution is the high-fidelity Pfu DNA polymerase, widely used in PCR because it makes fewer errors than Taq polymerase.

Geobacillus stearothermophilus: A heat-tolerant benchmark for sterilization.

Geobacillus stearothermophilus is a heat-loving, spore-forming bacterium with a complex taxonomic history. It was first grouped under Bacillus, but work by Nazina and colleagues (Nazina et al., 2001) helped define the separate Geobacillus genus. Recent studies (Burgess et al., 2017) suggest this group may still be split further, showing how difficult microbial classification can be. Beyond taxonomy, it is important in the food industry (Champidou et al., 2025) because its hardy spores can survive processing and contaminate products. For the same reason, its spores are widely used as biological indicators to confirm whether sterilization methods are effective.

Sulfolobus solfataricus: Shrugs off heat and acid yet loses to the little guy.

Sulfolobus solfataricus is a versatile extremophile that thrives in both very hot and highly acidic environments. Soon after its discovery, this archaeon became a model organism for understanding how life adapts to such harsh conditions. It was one of the first prokaryotes shown to have multiple replication origins (Soppa, 2005), and it uses a modified Entner-Doudoroff pathway for metabolism. Another unusual feature of Sulfolobus solfataricus is its susceptibility to a group of viruses adapted to infect (Wirth & Young, 2020) Sulfolobus species. Together, these interactions offer a glimpse into the unusual and still understudied world of archaeal viruses, and highlight how persistent both microbes and their viruses can be.
